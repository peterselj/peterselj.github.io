Things I write.

You should sign up for updates from me by email.

About once a month, I'll publish an essay on the most interesting thing I can think of. Subject matter spans business, pop culture, and the future. Short enough to read in a single sitting, long enough to provoke thoughtful dialogue.

I don't keep a blog anymore (I did for about 10 years). Today, my work is distributed exclusively by email, and lives entirely within Google Docs — an ecosystem where it's easier than anywhere else I could think of for me and my readers to comment (in-line) and converse (via Gmail).

My metric of choice is not "scale," or millions of viewers, or thousands of shares. I don't chase clicks. My metric is dialogue. In 2016-17, every 1,000 words I wrote turned into over 3,000 words in comments from about two dozen loyal readers. I'm proud of that.

If being a part of this conversation sounds interesting, again, you can sign up here. If you want to see a few samples first, you can check out the Featured Essays below.


Featured Essays.


Don't Sell Shovels: It's considered savvy business advice to say "If there's a gold rush, you want to be the one selling shovels." This seemed like bullshit. So I explored why.

But What if We're Wrong? Over the summer, Kevin and I read Chuck Klosterman's latest book, which explores strange futuristic ideas like "Where do you find the next Franz Kafka?" and "How will we think of Rock music in 500 years?" I highlighted and commented on 🔒 some of my favorite passages.




Global Warming is an enormous, problematic, confounding issue which may cause the end of the world as we know it. Can you solve Global Warming in 2,200 words? I give it a shot. Or, at least, I take a realistic look at how to get us started.




Soul Cycle is not why poor people are fat. An article came out on Vox casting blame on SoulCycle for the epidemic of crummy health among poor people. I thought the whole thing was counterproductive and missed the point entirely, so I dissected why, and proposed an alternative to how we might make a practical difference in the health and fitness of those around us.
